Neural Speech Coding With Timbre–Phoneme–Acoustic Representations for Scalable Intelligibility and Fidelity

Jiejie Guo, Student Member, IEEE, Wenjun Xu, Senior Member, IEEE, Fengyu Wang, Member, IEEE,
Junxiao Liang, Student Member, IEEE, JiJie Liu, Jiaqi Wang, Student Member, IEEE, and Ping Zhang, Fellow, IEEE
Abstract: Recent neural speech codecs have demonstrated impressive capabilities in synthesizing high-quality audio from discrete representations. However, prevailing methods rely on frame-level processing, which prevents them from approaching the theoretical compression limit of speech and lacks semantic scalability to seamlessly transition from intelligibility to high-fidelity within a single model. In this paper, a novel Timbre-Phoneme-Acoustic Neural Speech Codec (TPA-Codec) is proposed. In specific, first, a phoneme-level dynamic semantic compression mechanism is introduced, where phoneme boundaries are incorporated as structural priors to dynamically aggregate frame-level representations into phoneme-level units, drastically reducing the token rate. Furthermore, a semantic-guided hierarchical disentanglement framework is designed, decomposing speech into a three-level representation, i.e., a timbre layer modeling slow-varying timbre characteristics, a phoneme layer capturing core linguistic units, and an acoustic layer reconstructing high-frequency residual details. Consequently, TPA-Codec achieves intelligible reconstruction at around 60 bps and high-fidelity reconstruction at up to around 6 kbps, achieving seamless intelligibility-fidelity scalability in a single model.

Experimental Results

We compare the proposed method with state-of-the-art speech codecs from three categories. For traditional codecs, we employ MELPe evaluated at 0.6, 1.2, and 2.4 kbp, and Opus evaluated at 4 and 6 kbps. For single-rate neural codecs, we implement HIFI-Codec(GitHub) at 2 kbps, BigCodec(GitHub) at 1.04 kbps, U-Codec(GitHub) at 1.28 kbps, and LLM-Codec(GitHub) at 0.85 kbps. For multi-rate neural codecs, we include EnCodec(GitHub) evaluated at 1.5, 3, and 6 kbps, WavTokenizer(GitHub) evaluated at 0.5 and 0.9 kbps, Mimi(GitHub) from 137.5 bps to 1.1 kbps, SpeechTokenizer(GitHub) from 0.5 to 4 kbps, SemantiCodec(GitHub) evaluated at 0.35, 0.70, and 1.40 kbps, FlexiCodec(GitHub) evaluated at 113, 306, 225, and 149 bps, TiCodec(GitHub) evaluated at around 0.761, 1.511, and 3.011 kbps, and DAC(GitHub) tested across 0.5 to 6 kbps. Furthermore, we retrained their original architectures on the LibriSpeech train-clean-360 dataset for a fair comparison, denoting these specific baselines as DAC\dagger and TiCodec\dagger. To explore lower frame rates, we additionally modified their downsampling rates to [4, 4, 5, 8] and [2, 4, 4, 5, 8] to train variants operating at 25 Hz and 12.5 Hz respectively.

We select a subset of audio samples from datasets, presenting the original audio alongside reconstructions from various methods. Below each reconstructed audio, we display the recognized text obtained using the hubert-large-ls960-ft ASR model (HuggingFace).
