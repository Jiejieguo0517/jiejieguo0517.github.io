Can Speech Coding Reach 100 bps? A Phoneme-Level Semantic Codec

Jiejie Guo, Student Member, IEEE, Wenjun Xu, Senior Member, IEEE, Fengyu Wang, Member, IEEE, Junxiao Liang, Student Member, IEEE, JiJie Liu, Jiaqi Wang, Student Member, IEEE, Ping Zhang Fellow, IEEE
Abstract: Prevailing neural speech codecs rely on frame-level feature extraction and quantization, failing to effectively exploit the structural properties of speech—the discrete phonemic unit structure. This limitation leads to severe semantic intelligibility degradation at low bitrates. To address this, we propose the Phoneme-Level Semantic Codec (PLS-Codec), a neural speech coding framework that incorporates a phoneme-level dynamic compression mechanism. By integrating phoneme boundaries derived from the Montreal Forced Aligner (MFA) as structural priors, PLS-Codec dynamically aggregates frame-level features into phoneme-level representations, drastically reducing the token rate. As a result, PLS-Codec enables intelligible transmission at an ultra-low bitrate of 97 bps, which approaches the theoretical limit of speech coding.
Keywords: Speech coding, ultra-low bitrate, vector quantization.

Figure 1: PLS-Codec Architecture

Experimental Results

We compare our method with traditional low-bitrate codec MELPe at 0.6/1.2 kbps, and neural codecs including EnCodec (GitHub) at 1.5 kbps, the State-of-the-Art (SOTA) neural codec DAC (GitHub) at 0.5/1 kbps WavTokenizer (GitHub) at 0.5/0.9 kbps, and LLMCodec (GitHub) at 0.85 kbps. All methods are evaluated using their official checkpoints.

We select a subset of audio samples from each dataset, presenting the original audio alongside reconstructions from various methods. Below each reconstructed audio, we display the recognized text obtained using the hubert-large-ls960-ft ASR model (HuggingFace).
